Kanazawa vs Kyoto: The Honest Comparison

Do Kyoto first. Do Kanazawa second. Here's why.

Verdict

Kyoto first trip. Kanazawa second trip.

Getting There

2.5 hrs from Tokyo / 2 hrs from Kyoto

Budget

¥5,000–8,000/night

Stay

2 nights Kanazawa / 3–4 nights Kyoto

Insider Tips

  • Everything in Kanazawa is walkable. 20 minutes between all major sights. No bus needed.
  • Kenrokuen in December with yukitsuri (snow rope supports) under fresh snow is the best garden moment in Japan.
  • Omicho Ichibazushi (kaiten sushi inside the market) is the repeat-visitor pick for seafood.
  • Gyokuseninmaru Garden next to Kenrokuen is free and locals prefer it.
  • Winter: lower hotel prices, crab season, fewer tourists. The best time nobody visits.

Do Kyoto on your first trip to Japan. Do Kanazawa on your second. If you've already been to Kyoto and you're wondering whether Kanazawa is worth the detour: yes. It might be the highlight of your next trip.

Both cities survived WWII with their old quarters intact, so the wooden buildings and stone lanes are real, not reconstructions. But Kyoto was the emperor's capital and Kanazawa was a samurai domain. Different history, different food, different feel. This guide walks through who should pick each city, the numbers for both, and three itinerary scenarios depending on how many days you have.

Which city should I pick?

Never been to Japan? Do Kyoto. Fushimi Inari's torii gates, Kinkaku-ji, kaiseki dinner in Gion. These are singular experiences that only exist here. Kanazawa can wait.

Done Kyoto once, have 14 days? Do both. Thunderbird train between them, 2 hours. Kyoto first (3 nights), Kanazawa second (2 nights). The contrast makes both cities better.

Done Kyoto twice, have 7–10 days? Skip Kyoto this time. Kanazawa (2 nights) plus Takayama (2 nights) plus a Shirakawago day trip is a better use of limited days than retracing Kyoto's temple circuit.

Only have 1 spare day? Don't day-trip Kanazawa from Tokyo. It's 2.5 hours each way. Stay overnight or save it for the next trip.

The biggest practical difference: Kanazawa is walkable. Kenrokuen Garden, the Nagamachi samurai district, Higashi Chaya (geisha quarter), Omicho Market, and the 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art are all within a 20-minute walk of each other. In Kyoto, you're on a bus system that gets stuck in traffic and packed with tourists in peak season.

Where does Kanazawa win?

Crowds. Kenrokuen is one of Japan's three great gardens. On a Tuesday morning, you might share it with 20 people. Kyoto's Kinkaku-ji has 20 people in every photo you take. The adjacent Gyokuseninmaru Garden, which most visitors skip entirely, is free and locals actually prefer it.

Seafood. Kanazawa sits on the Sea of Japan. Omicho Market has sushi counters on the upper floor where the fish came off the boat that morning. Amaebi (sweet shrimp), nodoguro (blackthroat seaperch), buri (yellowtail). Lunch sets run ¥2,000–4,000. From November to March, snow crab (kano-gani) joins the menu. Omicho Ichibazushi, a kaiten spot inside the market, is the one people keep coming back to. Kyoto's Nishiki Market has become more of a tourist snacking street. Omicho is still where locals shop for dinner.

The surprise factor. The 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art is the most interesting art museum between Tokyo and Kyoto. A glass-disc building housing international installations, including Leandro Erlich's swimming pool. You don't expect contemporary art between samurai houses and geisha tearooms. The D.T. Suzuki Museum next door is less a museum than a meditation space: water, concrete, silence.

Where does Kyoto win?

Temples. This isn't close. Fushimi Inari's 10,000 torii gates at dawn. Kinkaku-ji. Kiyomizu-dera. Ryoan-ji's rock garden. Tofuku-ji in autumn. Kanazawa has Myoryuji (the "ninja temple," genuinely cool, book ahead for the guided tour) and a few neighborhood shrines. If temples are why you came to Japan, nothing substitutes for Kyoto.

Geisha culture. Kanazawa's Higashi Chaya has teahouses and gold leaf workshops. Kyoto's Gion has active maiko (apprentice geisha) and real ozashiki evenings. If this matters to you, there is only Kyoto.

Kaiseki. Multi-course traditional cuisine was invented in Kyoto. Kanazawa's food is excellent but different: seafood, not kaiseki. If a single kaiseki dinner is your trip's splurge moment, book it in Kyoto.

First-trip impact. Kyoto delivers moments that become the trip for someone who has never been to Japan. Fushimi Inari at 6am, matcha in a temple garden, a maiko crossing the street in Gion. These aren't replaceable. Kanazawa rewards the return visitor who already has that foundation.

Is Kanazawa getting crowded too?

Yes. The Hokuriku Shinkansen opened in 2015 and cut Tokyo to Kanazawa from four hours to 2.5. Tourism has climbed every year since. "Undiscovered" is no longer accurate.

But the scale is still different. Kyoto's Arashiyama bamboo grove is, by most recent accounts, overrated at midday. Hotel prices have climbed past ¥15,000 for basic rooms in peak season. Kanazawa's Kenrokuen might have 30 people at its signature viewpoint while Kyoto's equivalent has 300.

The gap is narrowing. If experiencing a traditional Japanese city without timed-entry tickets and crowd-management barriers matters to you, the time is now. Five years from now, the window may have closed.

How do I get there?

From Tokyo: Hokuriku Shinkansen, 2.5 hours. Take the Kagayaki (non-stop, reserved seats only) rather than the Hakutaka (more stops, but has unreserved cars).

From Kyoto or Osaka: Thunderbird limited express, about 2 hours. This is the train that connects the two cities on one trip.

Inside Kanazawa: Walk. Everything important is within a 20-minute radius. The loop bus exists but you won't need it for the main sights.

Continuing into the Alps: Nohi Bus from Kanazawa to Takayama (2 hours, ¥3,600). Shirakawago is a 75-minute bus ride from Kanazawa. Both connect naturally to the Alps circuit.

The Takayama-Hokuriku Tourist Pass (5 days) covers Osaka to Kanazawa, Kanazawa to Takayama, and Takayama to Nagoya. If your route touches those cities, it pays for itself by day two. Check current pricing and coverage before booking, as the Hokuriku Shinkansen extension has changed how you get between Kyoto/Osaka and Kanazawa.

If you have the days, put them back to back. Kyoto first (3 nights), then Thunderbird to Kanazawa (2 nights). The contrast hits when you step off the train: the pace drops, the streets clear, the sushi improves. The two cities complement each other better than they compete.

The December move nobody makes

Kanazawa in December, after the first snow. The garden staff at Kenrokuen install yukitsuri: rope supports fanned out from poles above each tree to protect branches from the weight of the snow. It looks like a giant art installation. The garden under fresh snow, with yukitsuri catching the light, is the single best garden moment in Japan. You'll share it with almost nobody.

Crab season runs through March. Hotel prices drop in winter. Pair it with a stop further north if you want the full snow country experience, or take the Thunderbird back to Kyoto for the holiday illuminations.

One more thing. Kanazawa produces 99% of Japan's gold leaf. You'll see it on soft serve and coffee everywhere. But if you collect goshuin (temple and shrine stamps), Kanazawa's shrines are the only place in Japan where the stamps come with real gold leaf pressed into the page. Ishiura Shrine near Kenrokuen does one of the best. About ¥500 per stamp. It's the kind of detail that turns a good trip into the one you keep talking about.
