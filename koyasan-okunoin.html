Okunoin at Dawn: Walking Koyasan's Cemetery

Stay overnight in a Buddhist temple. Walk the cemetery before sunrise. One night that changes how you see Japan.

Verdict

Stay overnight in a temple. Nothing else like it.

Getting There

2 hrs from Osaka / 2.5 hrs direct bus from Kyoto

Budget

¥10,000–15,000/night (temple stay with meals)

Stay

1 night minimum (2 if hiking Choishi Michi)

Insider Tips

  • Day trip is the #1 mistake — you miss the dawn cemetery, fire ceremony, and both shojin ryori meals.
  • Ekoin is the safe default for English speakers. Night cemetery tour and fire ceremony included.
  • Buy everything you need in Osaka before heading up — one convenience store, far from most temples.
  • Winter is extraordinary but pack layers — temple rooms have futons, not central heating.
  • Walk Okunoin from Ichinohashi Bridge (traditional entrance), not the Okunoin-mae bus stop shortcut.

Koyasan is the single best overnight experience in Japan. A mountaintop Buddhist monastery town where you sleep in a temple, eat what monks eat, walk a 1,200-year-old cemetery at dawn, and attend a fire ceremony before breakfast. It is two hours from Osaka and entirely accessible by train and cable car. It is also not a day trip. The travelers who try to squeeze Koyasan into a day from Osaka or Kyoto consistently report the same thing: they missed everything that makes it worth going.

The overnight stay is the experience. Without it, Koyasan is a collection of temples and a long graveyard. With it, you're eating shojin ryori by candlelight, watching mist settle over cedar trees from your tatami room, and walking Okunoin cemetery at 5:30am while the only sound is your own footsteps on stone.

Who should go to Koyasan?

Day trip from Osaka? Don't. The transit alone eats four hours round trip. You'll arrive around noon, rush through Okunoin, miss the cemetery at dawn, miss the fire ceremony, miss shojin ryori dinner, and leave wondering what the fuss was about. This is the single most common mistake people make with Koyasan.

One night available? Perfect. This is the ideal amount of time. Arrive early afternoon, explore Danjo Garan and the Kongobuji temple complex, eat temple dinner, walk Okunoin at night or at dawn, attend the morning fire ceremony, eat temple breakfast, and take the cable car back down by late morning.

Two nights? Add the Choishi Michi pilgrimage trail. This is the original path to Koyasan: 15 miles through the mountains from Kudoyama, marked by 180 stone pillars placed 800 years ago. About 6.5 hours of walking. It transforms the trip from a temple stay into a pilgrimage. Pair the second day with the Kumano Kodo if your schedule allows.

Traveling with kids? Probably skip this one. The temple curfews, futon sleeping, and long cemetery walks work best for adults. Families with older teenagers (who can handle a 9pm lights-out) do fine.

Why Koyasan?

Three things happen at Koyasan that happen nowhere else in Japan, and they all happen within 16 hours of each other.

The temple stay (shukubo). You sleep in a functioning Buddhist temple. Your room is tatami with sliding paper doors opening onto a private garden. Dinner and breakfast are shojin ryori, the vegetarian cuisine developed by monks over centuries. Even committed meat-eaters come away impressed. The food is precise, seasonal, and more flavorful than you'd expect from dishes built entirely around tofu, root vegetables, mountain herbs, and sesame. You eat it at a low table in your room, served course by course.

The cemetery. Okunoin is Japan's largest cemetery, with over 200,000 graves and memorial stones stretching along a 2km path through ancient cedar forest. Samurai lords, business empires, and ordinary families all have stones here, side by side, some covered in moss so thick the inscriptions have vanished. The path ends at the mausoleum of Kobo Daishi, the monk who founded Koyasan in 816 CE. Believers hold that he didn't die but entered eternal meditation. A flame has burned continuously at his hall for over 1,000 years.

The morning fire ceremony (goma). Before breakfast, monks chant sutras while feeding wooden prayer sticks into a ritual fire. The hall fills with incense smoke and the sound of drums and bells. You don't need to be Buddhist to feel the weight of it. You just sit and watch. Most temple stays include this in your booking.

What happens during a temple stay?

2:00-3:00pm: Check in. Most temples accept guests from early afternoon. You'll be shown to your tatami room, given a yukata, and offered green tea and a sweet. Some temples have garden views. Some face the forest. None have TVs.

3:00-5:00pm: Explore. Walk the Danjo Garan temple complex, the administrative heart of Koyasan. The Konpon Daito pagoda is the visual centerpiece. The Reihokan Museum has a world-class collection of Buddhist art. Both are within walking distance of most temples.

5:30-6:30pm: Dinner. Shojin ryori served in your room or in a communal dining hall. Multiple small dishes: sesame tofu (goma-dofu), tempura vegetables, pickled mountain vegetables, miso soup, rice. No meat, no fish, no garlic, no onion. The simplicity is the point. Many travelers call this the most memorable meal of their trip.

7:30-9:00pm: Evening walk or night tour. Some temples (Ekoin is the most popular) offer guided night walks through Okunoin cemetery. An English-speaking monk leads you along the lantern-lit path explaining the history of the graves. The combination of darkness, cedar trees, stone markers, and silence is unlike anything else in Japan. If your temple doesn't offer a night tour, you can still walk Okunoin independently after dark. The path is lit. You won't be alone, but you'll feel like it.

9:00pm: Lights out. Some temples have curfews as early as 9pm. Check before booking if this matters to you. There is no nightlife on Koyasan. This is the point.

6:00-6:30am: Morning ceremony. The goma fire ritual. Attendance is optional but strongly recommended. You'll sit in the temple's main hall on cushions while monks chant. The ceremony runs 30-45 minutes. Dress warmly in winter.

7:00-7:30am: Breakfast. Another shojin ryori meal, lighter than dinner. Rice porridge, pickles, tofu, miso soup. Then you pack, check out, and have the rest of the morning to walk Okunoin at dawn before heading down the mountain.

What should I know about Okunoin?

The cemetery runs roughly 2km from Ichinohashi Bridge to the Lantern Hall at Kobo Daishi's mausoleum. The path is paved stone, flanked by cedar trees that are 200-600 years old. The graves range from massive samurai monuments to tiny jizo statues wearing red bibs.

Dawn vs night. Both are worth doing if you have the chance. At night, the stone lanterns along the path create pools of warm light between the trees, and the mausoleum at the end glows with thousands of oil lamps. At dawn, mist hangs between the cedars and the only sounds are birds and the occasional monk sweeping leaves. Dawn is quieter. Night is more atmospheric. If you can only do one, dawn is the consensus pick. Walk from the Ichinohashi Bridge end (the traditional entrance), not the Okunoin-mae bus stop shortcut.

What you'll see along the path. Corporate memorial stones from companies like Panasonic and Nissan. A rocket-shaped memorial from an aerospace company. Termite exterminators with a monument apologizing to the insects. Samurai gravestones with family crests. A memorial for those who died in WWII. The path is a cross-section of Japanese society spanning centuries, and the juxtaposition of corporate logos next to 400-year-old moss-covered stone is part of what makes it unlike any other cemetery.

Photography is allowed along the main path but prohibited in the innermost area around the Lantern Hall and mausoleum. Respect the signs.

Which temple should I book?

Ekoin. The most popular choice for international visitors. An English-speaking monk leads the night cemetery tour, which is included with your stay. The fire ceremony is open to guests. Rooms are well-maintained. Books up weeks in advance, especially in autumn. This is the safe default.

Fukuchiin. The rare temple stay that includes its own onsen. If soaking in a hot spring bath after walking the cemetery sounds right, this is your temple. The onsen experience here is smaller and simpler than a dedicated onsen town, but having it inside a temple compound is unusual.

Shojoshin-in. Quieter and less touristed than Ekoin. Recommended by travelers who found Ekoin too busy or too English-language-oriented. The garden is considered one of the best on the mountain. No night tour, so you'd walk Okunoin independently.

Budget alternative: Hotel Tommy. Not a temple stay. A standard small Japanese inn on Koyasan's main street. About ¥5,000-7,000/night without meals. You'd eat at one of the town's restaurants instead. Hanabishi is one of the few reliably open for lunch. This works if you want the cemetery and the town without the shukubo format, but understand that you're giving up the core experience.

How do I get there and what does it cost?

From Osaka (Namba): Nankai Railway to Gokurakubashi Station (about 90 minutes), then the cable car up the mountain (5 minutes), then a local bus to your temple or the town center (10-15 minutes). Total: about 2 hours door to door. The Koyasan World Heritage Ticket (around ¥3,400) covers the round-trip train, cable car, and unlimited bus rides on the mountain. It saves roughly ¥1,000 over buying tickets separately and removes all the decision-making from the transit.

From Kyoto: A direct bus runs from Kyoto Station to Koyasan. About 2.5 hours, around ¥2,500 one way. This avoids the Osaka transfer entirely and drops you in the center of town. Seats are limited, so book ahead in peak season. The alternative is train to Osaka Namba, then the Nankai route above.

From Nara: No direct connection. Go via Osaka Namba (about 1 hour Nara to Namba on Kintetsu, then the Nankai route). Total 2.5-3 hours.

On the mountain: Local buses connect all the main sites and temple lodgings. The town is also walkable end to end in about 30 minutes, but the distances between sites can add up. One convenience store exists but it's a long walk from most temples. Buy anything you need in Osaka before heading up.

Cost breakdown for one night:

Item Cost
Koyasan World Heritage Ticket (round trip from Osaka) ¥3,400
Temple stay with dinner and breakfast ¥10,000–15,000
Reihokan Museum entry ¥1,300
Kongobuji Temple entry ¥1,000
Lunch (Hanabishi or similar) ¥1,000–1,500
Total ¥16,700–22,200

Okunoin cemetery is free. The fire ceremony is free (included with your temple stay). The night tour at Ekoin is free for guests. The most important experiences on Koyasan cost nothing beyond the room.

What do most people get wrong?

Day-tripping. Already covered above, but worth repeating: Koyasan without the overnight stay is a different (and worse) experience. The transit time alone makes a day trip feel rushed. You miss the cemetery at dawn and at night, the fire ceremony, and both shojin ryori meals. If you only have a day, spend it somewhere else and save Koyasan for a trip where you can stay.

Not checking the curfew. Some temples lock the doors at 9pm. If you planned to walk Okunoin at night independently and your temple closes at 9, you have a problem. Ask before booking. Ekoin's night tour returns before curfew. Other temples vary.

Expecting restaurants. Koyasan has a handful of restaurants, and several close early or don't open at all in winter. Hanabishi is the reliable option for lunch. But the town is not set up for eating out. Your meals are at your temple. If you're staying at Hotel Tommy or another non-temple option, plan your meals carefully. There is no convenience store near most accommodations.

Autumn weekends. Koyasan's maple trees turn in late October and November. The mountain fills up with domestic tourists, especially on weekends. Temple stays book out weeks ahead. If autumn is your only option, book early and go midweek. Spring and early summer are less crowded and the cedar forest is green and cool.

Packing too light for winter. Koyasan sits at 800 meters elevation. It snows from December through February. The temple rooms have futons and sometimes kerosene heaters but are not centrally heated. The corridor between your room and the bathroom will be cold. Bring layers. The winter atmosphere is extraordinary, but so is the chill.

Beyond Okunoin: the Choishi Michi trail

If you have a second night on Koyasan, the Choishi Michi is the reason to use it. This is the original pilgrimage path to the mountaintop, used for over 1,000 years before the cable car existed. It starts at Kudoyama Station (reachable by Nankai Railway from Osaka) and climbs through mountain forest for 24 kilometers, passing 180 stone markers (choishi) placed at roughly 109-meter intervals.

The walk takes about 6-7 hours. It is not technical but it is long and involves steady uphill. The trail passes through forest for nearly its entire length, with occasional clearings and small shrines. You emerge at the Daimon Gate, the western entrance to Koyasan, having arrived the way pilgrims have for centuries. The sense of arrival is different from stepping off a cable car.

For travelers building a longer Kansai itinerary, Koyasan connects naturally to the Kumano Kodo pilgrimage trails further south. The two share a spiritual lineage and a geographic overlap on the Kii Peninsula. Walking both in one trip is the deep-Kansai experience that the region page is built around. Consider Kinosaki Onsen as a recovery stop between the two, or pair Koyasan with the Alps circuit if you're heading north after Osaka.

One more thing. At the far end of the Okunoin path, just before the Lantern Hall, there is a small stone basin called the Miroku Stone. Visitors try to lift it from a shelf inside the stone housing. Tradition holds that if you can lift it easily, your wishes will come true. Almost nobody can. The monks will tell you it weighs about 20 kilograms, but it feels heavier than that. It's the kind of small, strange moment that sticks with you longer than the grand ones.
